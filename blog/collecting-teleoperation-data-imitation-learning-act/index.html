Collecting Teleoperation Data for Imitation Learning on a Consumer GPU with ACT

June 9, 2026 · Prometheus Robotics

Imitation learning turns human demonstrations into robot policies. You teleoperate the robot through a task a few dozen times, record what it saw and what it did, and train a model to reproduce the behaviour. The appealing part for most labs: with the right policy, the whole loop — collection and training — fits on a single consumer GPU.

ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers) is the policy that made this practical. It is small, sample-efficient, and trains comfortably on a 24 GB card. This guide walks through collecting clean teleoperation data on the Prometheus humanoid and training an ACT policy end-to-end, without a cluster.

Why ACT is a great consumer-GPU baseline

ACT was introduced with the ALOHA project (Zhao et al., 2023) to learn fine-grained manipulation from cheap hardware. Two ideas make it efficient:

  • Action chunking. Instead of predicting one action at a time, ACT predicts a chunk of the next k actions (often k≈100). This shortens the effective horizon and fights compounding error.
  • Temporal ensembling. At inference, overlapping chunks are averaged, producing smooth, stable motion.

The model itself is a compact CVAE with a Transformer encoder–decoder — on the order of tens of millions of parameters. In practice you can train a task on a single RTX 3090/4090 in a few hours from roughly 50 demonstrations. That is the whole reason ACT remains the first thing to try before reaching for a large vision-language-action model.

What you actually record

An imitation-learning episode is a synchronized stream of observations and actions sampled at a fixed rate (30–50 Hz works well). On Prometheus that maps cleanly onto the onboard sensing:

  • Vision: the head-mounted stereo pair for full-scene context plus the wrist cameras for close-in manipulation. The wrist mounting angle is adjustable, which matters — keep it consistent across every demo.
  • Proprioception: joint positions for the arms, grippers, and any active modules (the platform exposes 19 DoF on the upper body, more with hands and legs).
  • Actions: the target joint commands you send while teleoperating — what the policy learns to output.

Teleoperating Prometheus to collect demos

Prometheus ships with the teleoperation pipeline out of the box, so there is nothing to integrate before you can record:

  • VR teleop with a Meta Quest 3S — intuitive for full-arm reaching and grasping, and the most natural way to demonstrate manipulation.
  • Leader–follower / manual controller for precise, repeatable trajectories.

Each recorded run becomes one episode. Store them in the LeRobot dataset format — the de-facto standard for ACT and most open imitation-learning stacks — so the same data feeds any policy later.

# Illustrative — record teleop episodes into a LeRobot dataset
python -m lerobot.record \
    --robot.type=prometheus \
    --teleop.type=quest3s \
    --dataset.repo_id=yourlab/pick_place_cube \
    --dataset.num_episodes=50 \
    --dataset.fps=30

Data quality beats data quantity. 50 clean, consistent demonstrations train a better policy than 200 sloppy ones. Vary object position and lighting on purpose, keep the camera framing fixed, and — importantly — include recoveries: let the gripper miss and then correct, so the policy learns to get back on track instead of freezing.

Training ACT on one GPU

Once the dataset exists, training is a single command. ACT reads the multi-camera images and joint states, and learns to output action chunks:

# Illustrative — train ACT from the recorded dataset
python -m lerobot.train \
    --policy.type=act \
    --dataset.repo_id=yourlab/pick_place_cube \
    --batch_size=8 \
    --steps=100000 \
    --output_dir=outputs/act_pick_place

On a 24 GB consumer card this runs in a few hours; 12 GB is enough if you trim batch size and image resolution. No multi-node setup, no rented A100s — which is exactly what makes ACT the right starting point for a single workstation.

Deploying the policy back on the robot

Inference is light: ACT runs in real time on the onboard compute (Raspberry Pi 5 / NVIDIA Jetson) or a tethered laptop, streaming action chunks to the robot through the Prometheus SDK and its simple REST API. Because you validated everything against the bundled simulator and URDF first, the sim-to-real gap is small.

When to graduate to a VLA

ACT is excellent at a specific task it has seen. The moment you need a policy that generalizes across objects, instructions, and scenes it has never been demonstrated on, you want a pretrained vision-language-action model that you fine-tune on the same teleoperation data. The good news: the dataset you collected here transfers directly.

That is the subject of the companion guide — same robot, same data pipeline, a far more capable policy.
